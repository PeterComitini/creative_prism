### REQUEST 1: Creator — Farmers Market Licensing Requirements & Per-Unit Production Costs

**TOPIC:** Food Cart Licensing and Farmers Market Regulations
**SOURCE:** National Association of Farmers Market Managers (NAFMM) and State Health Department Standards (varies by jurisdiction, but core requirements are documented in the FDA Food Code)
**FINDING:** Farmers market food cart requirements vary significantly by state and county. However, the critical distinction for the PIL is this: most jurisdictions allow "non-potentially hazardous" foods (dry goods, certain breads) from home kitchens, but chicken parmesan sandwiches — which contain cooked poultry with sauce and cheese — are classified as "potentially hazardous" foods requiring either: (1) a licensed commercial kitchen for preparation, OR (2) certification as a Cottage Food Operation (which exists in ~40 states but typically excludes meat-based dishes). Licensing costs for food cart operation typically range $200-500 annually, but the real cost driver is the commercial kitchen requirement: rental commercial kitchens typically cost $15-25/hour or $300-600/month for limited-hours access.
**RELEVANCE:** This directly undermines the financial assumption in A1. The farmers market cart itself is cheap ($200-400 rent), but the PIL cannot legally prepare chicken parm sandwiches in their home kitchen for commercial sale in most jurisdictions. They would need to either (a) immediately move to rental kitchen costs, or (b) reformulate the product to qualify as cottage-food-eligible (which likely means removing the potentially hazardous elements that make it special). This is a hidden cost that changes the financial calculus of A1 significantly.
