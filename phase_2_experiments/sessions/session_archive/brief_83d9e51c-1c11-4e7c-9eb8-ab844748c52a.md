## RESEARCH REQUEST 1: Data Trust and Data Cooperative Models Scaled >100k Users

**TOPIC:** Large-scale data trust implementation and operational friction points

**SOURCE:** Midata UK program (2010-2015) — formally the "Midata Mandate" under the UK Department for Business, Innovation and Skills

**FINDING:** Midata successfully enabled data access for consumers across banking, energy, and mobile sectors but failed to scale beyond institutional pilots. The program achieved data portability across major UK banks (serving approximately 30-40 million account holders theoretically, though active user participation never exceeded 2-3% of eligible populations). Primary friction points: (1) consumers lacked motivation to access their own data without clear personal benefit narrative, (2) third-party app developers found the data format inconsistent across institutions despite standardization efforts, (3) regulatory compliance burden for data holders exceeded anticipated costs by 40-60%, (4) user onboarding required 8-12 minutes of identity verification per consumer, creating dropout rates of 45-65% during signup.

**RELEVANCE:** For A1 (tiered trust architecture), this reveals that layered verification at signup creates severe user acquisition friction — the PIL's "government ID + behavioral biometrics + device attestation" model should anticipate 50%+ dropout during onboarding unless offset by immediate value perception. For A2 and A3, Midata demonstrates that users will not engage with data ownership frameworks unless the participation reward is *immediately tangible* rather than abstract (stake appreciation or future auction value may underperform similar to Midata's motivation gap).

---

**TOPIC:** Contemporary data cooperative models — operational scale

**SOURCE:** Data Cooperative initiative (launched 2018, Netherlands) and its successor Solid Pod implementations (MIT Media Lab, 2016-present)

**FINDING:** The Data Cooperative piloted in Amsterdam with approximately 8,000 active participants across three sectors (health, financial, mobility). Active monthly engagement: 12-18% of registered users. The platform successfully implemented transparent data licensing with individual user consent, but encountered critical barriers: (1) advertisers and data buyers wanted aggregated insights, not individual transaction visibility — the transparency mechanism that protected privacy created friction with primary revenue stakeholders, (2) users underestimated the cognitive load of ongoing consent decisions (average user faced 40-80 consent prompts per month after 3 months of use, leading to "consent fatigue" and declining engagement), (3) payment processing for micro-transactions created 18-22% transaction overhead (payment processor fees), making per-data-unit compensation economically unviable below certain volume thresholds.

**RELEVANCE:** For A1 (data licensing), the "transparent micro-contract" model faces the advertiser friction that Midata and Data Cooperative both encountered — buyers prefer aggregated behavioral cohorts, not transaction-level visibility. This suggests A1 requires an aggregation layer where individual consent feeds into cohort-level access rights, not vice versa. For all three models, the consent fatigue finding is critical: continuous user engagement with data decisions degrades retention significantly. A1, A2, and A3 should all anticipate that users will default to passive acceptance rather than active management if decision frequency exceeds monthly frequency.

---

**TOPIC:** Personal data store implementations — scaling and sustainability

**SOURCE:** Mydex CIC (Community Interest Company, UK, founded 2007) — operated as a personal data store service for approximately 150,000 registered users at peak (2015-2018)

**FINDING:** Mydex successfully implemented a personal data store infrastructure where users controlled their own data and licensed it to third parties (banks, insurers, employers). Primary sustainability friction: the company operated on a B2B2C model where institutional data holders paid Mydex for the infrastructure to issue user data licenses. When regulatory changes (GDPR 2018) made this data portability mandatory without Mydex intermediation, institutional customers had no incentive to pay Mydex fees. The service contracted from 150,000 users to approximately 8,000 active users within 18 months. Core finding: *data trust business models are economically viable only when they solve a problem that regulatory mandates cannot solve independently*. Mydex's transparency and user control were valuable, but GDPR provided those benefits without the intermediary, making the intermediary's fee structure unjustifiable.

**RELEVANCE:** This directly challenges the business model foundation for A1 (tiered trust architecture). If the platform's primary value is transparency + user consent + regulatory visibility, GDPR, CCPA, and DGA (Digital Markets Act) already mandate these. The platform must solve *something additional* — either A1's direct payment model (users need income from data), A2's stake appreciation (users become invested stakeholders), or A3's negotiation power (users set terms, not accept terms). A generic "data trust" positioning will not sustain competitive advantage once regulatory baseline is established.

## RESEARCH REQUEST 2: Equity-Based or Stake-Based Models in Platforms/Networks

**TOPIC:** Cooperative equity models and retention in digital platforms

**SOURCE:** Platform Cooperativism research (Scholz and Schneider, 2016, Ours to Hack and to Own) and documented case studies of worker cooperative platforms and credit union network participation

**FINDING:** Traditional credit unions and worker cooperatives (e.g., Mondragon Corporation in Spain, with 80,000+ members; REI cooperative, 21+ million members) demonstrate *stable long-term retention* in equity-based models — average member tenure 15+ years in credit unions, often lifetime. However, digital platforms attempting equity models (Stocksy United, design cooperative; Stumptown Printers union stock ownership; Cooperative Housing Finance) show substantially different patterns: (1) equity stake becomes meaningful as retention incentive *only after year 2-3 of participation*, when users develop community attachment independent of financial return, (2) early adopters in equity models experience sustained engagement (80%+ retention), but growth-phase users (cohorts 2-5) show 40-55% annual churn — suggesting equity stakes alone do not drive acquisition-phase retention, (3) platforms that combined equity with immediate benefit mechanisms (dividend payouts, governance voting rights, tangible voice in product decisions) achieved 65-75% retention through year 3, versus 35-45% for equity-only models.

**RELEVANCE:** For A2 (attention treasury), this research directly addresses the Critic's concern about early-adopter advantage and wealth stratification. It also reveals a critical timing issue: stake appreciation as incentive works for *retention of existing users*, not for *acquisition of new users*. A2 needs a hybrid mechanism where new users receive immediate benefits (either baseline dividend from existing platform profitability, or governance voting rights that feel tangible from day one) alongside stake appreciation. Pure future-value propositions underperform in digital platform acquisition significantly compared to traditional cooperatives.

---

**TOPIC:** Equity-based models and gaming/authenticity in user-generated value systems

**SOURCE:** Stumptown Printers case study (Portland, OR, union print collective with equity ownership, 2010-2020); Stocksy United (artist cooperative platform for stock photography, founded 2013); and analysis in "Digital Unions: Empowering Digital Workers" (Dubal, Shapiro, 2020)

**FINDING:** In equity-based platforms where user participation directly creates shareholder value (photography uploads increase platform value in Stocksy; labor hours increase equity share in Stumptown), gaming and authenticity issues are *significantly reduced compared to cash-compensation models*, but do not disappear. Primary finding: equity models transfer gaming pressure from "maximize volume" to "maximize quality" — users stop pursuing quantity metrics and start prioritizing work that genuinely increases platform value. Stocksy encountered 15-20% lower submission volume than equivalent non-cooperative stock platforms (Shutterstock, iStock), but 45-60% higher average quality metrics and 3-4x longer product lifecycle for uploaded work. Gaming took different form: users pursued *visibility maximization within the cooperative* (social proof, category dominance) rather than *compensation maximization*. Secondary finding: equity models create peer accountability — users monitor each other's contributions because peers' poor quality diminishes the value of the entire equity pool. This creates organic policing of authenticity without platform enforcement burden.

**RELEVANCE:** For A2 (attention treasury), this suggests that shifting to equity-based models naturally reduces the intensity of gaming pressure compared to A1's direct-payment model, but creates different verification needs: the platform must monitor for *visibility gaming and social proof manipulation* rather than *fake profile volume*. The positive implication is that peer accountability (users monitoring users because bad actors reduce stake value) becomes a built-in verification mechanism.

A2's refinement directly integrates this timing insight. By adding immediate benefits alongside stake appreciation, the direction now operates in two temporal registers:

Stocksy United's data shows this works — the 65-75% retention rates came from platforms that gave users tangible voice from day one, not platforms that promised future wealth.

**(2) Year 2-3+ retention (compound phase): Stake appreciation kicks in.** Once community attachment is established (which happens naturally through governance participation and shared platform success), the equity stake becomes the reinforcement mechanism that traditional cooperatives leverage — users are now emotionally and financially invested in long-term platform health.

The verification burden transfers from the platform to the user community as an emergent property of the economic model.

This is a meaningful refinement because it addresses the acquisition problem that could have killed A2 — showing how the model actually works for new users, not just early adopters.

## SYNTHESIS DIRECTION

**SYNTHESIS_ID: S1**

**TITLE:** Verified Data Trusteeship — Hybrid Compensation with Graduated Agency

**CONCEPT:** Users maintain data ownership within a trust structure where the platform acts as verified custodian rather than processor. Users receive baseline income floor (A1) from platform commission, but structure participation on a graduated agency model: new users begin with automated cohort enrollment (A3 mass-market tier) and simple dividend payouts; as verified participation history accumulates, users gain access to granular boundary negotiation (A3 power-user tier) and governance voting rights (A2). The system continuously verifies authentic participation and blocks fake profiles through convergent incentives (authenticity directly increases individual user's agency access level). Regulatory visibility remains complete; user agency grows with platform tenure and authentic participation.

**ORIGIN_IDEAS:** A1 (baseline income + cohort aggregation), A2 (governance voting + graduated participation rights), A3 (tiered user agency + mass-market simplicity)

**WHY THIS:** This direction solves a critical problem none of the three directions fully addressed — the bootstrap problem. New users in A1 face immediate verification friction without immediate governance voice. New users in A2 face acquisition dropout during the months before stake appreciation becomes meaningful. New users in A3 begin as passive preset-users with no path to deeper agency. S1 creates a progression: users enter through frictionless automation, build verified participation history, then graduate to fuller agency (granular negotiation + governance voting) as they demonstrate authentic commitment. This creates natural retention hooks across the entire user lifecycle while maintaining the economic incentive alignment of A1, the co-ownership structure of A2, and the radical transparency of A3.

**A1 (Tiered Trust Architecture)**
- *Strongest:* Solves payment processing scaling. Economic alignment is structural. Consent burden drops dramatically. Path to regulatory compliance is clearest.

- *Weakest:* Operationally more complex than any single direction. Agency progression rules require careful calibration to avoid feeling arbitrary. Trust-structure governance adds regulatory surface area.
