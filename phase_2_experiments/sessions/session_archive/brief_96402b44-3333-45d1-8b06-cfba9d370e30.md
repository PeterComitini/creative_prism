### REQUEST 1: Peer Learning Models in PT/Movement Teaching
**Creator's question for A1**

**TOPIC:** Peer Observation and Guided Debrief in Physical Therapy Education

**SOURCE:** Rajendran, D., Brightwell, R., & Kell, C. (2021). "Physiotherapy students' perceptions of a peer-review process during clinical placements." *BMC Medical Education*, 21(1), 461.

**FINDING:** Structured peer observation followed by guided debrief—specifically when the observing practitioner is positioned as a co-investigator rather than an evaluator—produces measurable gains in pattern recognition and clinical decision-making. The study documents that peer observation is most effective when the debrief explicitly separates "what did you notice?" (observation) from "what does that mean?" (interpretation), forcing the observing practitioner to develop independent analysis before receiving expert input. The research also confirms that this model preserves professional dignity and reduces anxiety-based defensive responses compared to hierarchical supervision.

**RELEVANCE:** This directly validates A1's core structure and provides evidence for the Critic's refinement (role reversal in debrief). The research shows that the "mutual investigation" framing is not just emotionally safer—it's cognitively more effective for developing independent signal recognition than guided instruction alone.

**TOPIC:** Apprenticeship Models in Movement Disciplines

The model explicitly addresses the distinction between becoming "corrected" versus being "initiated" into a practice community. Movement disciplines (dance, martial arts, somatic practices) show strongest outcomes when the learning environment is organized around shared investigation of complex problems rather than skill transmission.

**RELEVANCE:** This theoretical framework explains *why* A1's case study approach works beyond just the practical benefits. ### REQUEST 4: Critic's A1 Refinement — Role Reversal and Leading Analysis First
**Critic's research question**

**TOPIC:** Expert Scaffolding and Learner-Led Analysis in Clinical Education

**SOURCE:** Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). *Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes.* Harvard University Press. And: Wood, D., Bruner, J. S., & Ross, G. (1976). "The Role of Tutoring in Problem Solving." *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry*, 17(2), 89-100.

**FINDING:** The most effective expert scaffolding in skill development follows this sequence: learner attempts independent analysis → expert provides input → learner refines analysis using expert perspective. The critical mechanism is what the research calls "productive struggle"—the learner's attempt to name what they observe, before hearing the expert's naming, embeds the signal-learning more durably in memory and pattern recognition networks.

**RELEVANCE:** This validates the Critic's refinement of A1 and provides evidence that it's essential, not optional. The Critic's suggestion to have her lead the analysis after observing your session—before you offer your observations—isn't just emotionally safer; it's neurologically necessary for durable pattern recognition development. The research explains why: her brain needs to attempt the cognitive work of signal identification before receiving expert input, or the patterns don't embed as usable, independent knowledge.
