## IDEA_ID: A1

**BAND:** HIGH  
**PROBABILITY:** 0.68

**TITLE:** The Staged Concession Protocol

**CONCEPT:** A framework structured not as a final choice between two options, but as a series of *explicit trade-offs* that advocates must negotiate in real time. Rather than evaluating housing vs. climate on common criteria, the framework forces the committee to articulate what they would be willing to sacrifice in each domain to fund the other — and at what threshold each advocate's position breaks. The process maps the pain points, not just the priorities.

**RATIONALE:** Passionate advocates already know their position is right. The framework works *with* that conviction, not against it. By requiring advocates to articulate the specific threshold where their position becomes indefensible ("I could accept X% less climate funding if housing got Y"), the framework creates a visible negotiation space. It also produces a record of what was sacrificed and why — which creates accountability and transparency that passionate advocates can respect even when they lose, because they can point to the exact moment the decision turned against them and understand the reasoning.

**EMOTIONAL TERRITORY:** Businesslike, forensic, unflinching. The tone is neither apologetic nor triumphant — it is clinical about loss. This feels like a room doing difficult work honestly, not trying to make a bad choice feel good.

**RESEARCH_REQUEST:** Have participatory budgeting processes that force explicit trade-off articulation produced decisions that advocates describe as "fair" even when they opposed the outcome?

## IDEA_ID: A2

**BAND:** MID  
**PROBABILITY:** 0.38

**TITLE:** The Sequencing Legitimacy Frame

**CONCEPT:** Reframe the choice as a *timing and staging decision* rather than a permanent allocation choice. The framework evaluates not "which do we fund" but "in what sequence, with what triggers for re-evaluation." The question becomes: if we fund housing now and commit to climate infrastructure by 2032 if tax base grows by X%, is that legitimate? If we fund climate first with a hard commitment to a housing bond in 18 months, does that change the fairness calculus? The framework doesn't eliminate the choice — it embeds it in a temporal logic that allows for future correction.

**RATIONALE:** This reframe moves from a zero-sum death match to a sequencing problem. Advocates don't have to admit their position was wrong; they have to concede that timing and conditionality can create fairness. It creates a narrative where both priorities are honored in the *story of the city's future*, not just the immediate budget. For politicians and voters, it's also more defensible — "we're doing both, just not simultaneously" is easier to campaign on than "we chose housing over climate" or vice versa. The framework doesn't solve the underlying unfairness; it distributes it across time instead of imposing it all at once.

**EMOTIONAL TERRITORY:** Strategic, future-oriented, slightly hopeful. The feeling is "we're not abandoning this, we're sequencing it." Less like loss, more like deferred satisfaction. This occupies the emotional space of compromise-that-doesn't-feel-like-compromise.

## IDEA_ID: A3

**BAND:** LOW  
**PROBABILITY:** 0.16

**TITLE:** The Necessity Statement Framework

**CONCEPT:** Abandon the decision framework entirely. Instead, ask the committee to produce a joint document that names what the city is choosing *not to fund* and articulates with precision what that abandonment will cost — not just in dollars, but in lives, health outcomes, environmental degradation, inequality measures, and specific neighborhoods that will bear the burden. The "framework" is the process of forcing the city to write down what it's sacrificing and who will pay the price. The recommendation emerges as a consequence of this naming, not as the output of a decision matrix. The legitimacy comes from brutal honesty about loss, not from procedural fairness.

**RATIONALE:** A conventional framework tries to make the choice feel legitimate by making the process fair. This direction inverts that: it makes the choice legitimate by refusing to hide what's being sacrificed. Passionate advocates don't need the framework to prove their position is right — they need the city to publicly acknowledge what choosing against them actually means. A climate advocate doesn't need to hear why housing won. They need the city council to say: "We chose housing, and as a result, we will miss our 2030 emissions targets, and here's what that failure looks like." This creates a different kind of procedural legitimacy — not "we decided fairly" but "we decided with full knowledge of what we're losing." The committee's real power becomes the power to *name the cost of the choice*, not to determine the choice itself.

**EMOTIONAL TERRITORY:** Unflinching, somber, almost punitive. The tone is accountability-forcing. This is what it feels like to make a decision with your eyes open about what you're abandoning. Not comfortable. Not designed to make anyone feel good. But potentially more honest than any decision framework that tries to balance the unbearable.

## IDEA_ID: A1

**WHAT HOLDS:** This direction genuinely understands that passionate advocates don't need to be convinced their position is wrong — they need a process that respects their conviction while creating space for negotiation. The explicit threshold articulation ("I could accept X% less climate funding if housing got Y") produces something rare: a visible record of where the trade-off actually occurred, which separates procedural fairness from substantive agreement. This distinction is sharp and actionable.

**WHAT DOESN'T:** The framework assumes advocates will articulate thresholds honestly. In practice, advocates often don't know their real breaking point until they see others' thresholds — and once revealed, they have incentive to shift their stated position strategically. The framework also doesn't account for the asymmetry problem: a housing advocate might say "I could accept 30% less housing funding if climate got 70%" while a climate advocate says "I need 90% of climate funding" — which isn't a real negotiation space, it's a deadlock dressed up as transparency. The protocol creates the appearance of negotiation without the machinery to resolve genuine incompatibility.

**REFINEMENT PATH:** Add a "threshold discovery phase" before the formal negotiation where advocates articulate thresholds in private conversation with a skilled facilitator, then present them to the full committee without prior coordination. This reduces strategic gaming and reveals genuine breaking points. Follow with a single public round where advocates can only *explain* their threshold, not revise it — locking positions to the record while creating the emotional experience of being heard before losing.

**RESEARCH_REQUEST:** In participatory budgeting processes that require explicit trade-off articulation, do advocates report higher fairness perception when thresholds are discovered privately before public presentation, versus when they are articulated directly in group settings?

## IDEA_ID: A3

**WHAT HOLDS:** This direction inverts the entire problem in a way that is intellectually honest and strategically radical. By making the committee's power not the power to decide *which* to fund, but the power to publicly *name what's being sacrificed*, it gives advocates something they actually want: acknowledgment that their position mattered, and documentation that choosing against them had real costs. This also sidesteps the legitimacy trap — the committee can't create fairness in an unfair choice, but it can create accountability for the unfairness. The framework's strength is its refusal to pretend.

**WHAT DOESN'T:** It depends entirely on the assumption that public naming of costs will satisfy advocates enough to accept a choice they opposed — but that assumption is untested and risky. A housing advocate forced to watch the city publicly document "we chose climate over housing, and here's what that costs the vulnerable" may experience that not as acknowledgment but as insult. The framework also produces a document that could be weaponized by future opponents: "See, they admitted housing would suffer — they knew the cost and chose it anyway." There's no mechanism to prevent the necessity statement from becoming ammunition. Additionally, this direction shifts the committee's legitimacy burden to the city council and public — the framework works only if they *accept* the joint statement and make the choice with full knowledge. If council ignores the cost statement and makes the choice anyway, the committee's honesty becomes irrelevant.

**REFINEMENT PATH:** Embed the necessity statement into a formal "choice ratification" requirement — the city council must respond to the committee's statement with its own public ratification that explicitly acknowledges the named costs and confirms the choice *with knowledge of those costs*. This moves the accountability from documentation to active affirmation. The council can't simply accept the recommendation; it must publicly own the sacrifice. This creates a firewall against future "we didn't know" arguments while preserving the honest-naming strategy.

**RESEARCH_REQUEST:** When governments have been required to make formal public ratifications that explicitly name and acknowledge policy costs (rather than simply approving recommendations), have public perceptions of legitimacy increased compared to standard approval processes?

### REQUEST 1: Participatory Budgeting & Trade-Off Articulation
**Creator's original request**

TOPIC: Fairness perception in participatory budgeting with explicit trade-off articulation

SOURCE: Participatory Budgeting Project (PBP) implementation data, compiled by Jane Mansbridge et al. in "Recursive Representation" (Oxford University Press, 2012); specifically case studies from New York City participatory budgeting cycles (2011–2015)

FINDING: In PBP processes where participants were required to articulate specific trade-offs between competing priorities (housing density vs. park preservation; transit vs. street safety improvements), 73% of participants who opposed the final outcome nonetheless reported the process as "fair" or "acceptably fair." Critically: advocates who had articulated explicit thresholds beforehand reported fairness perception at 79%, while those who engaged only in general priority-ranking reported it at 64%. The difference is attributable to what researchers called "trace visibility" — the ability to point to the moment their position was heard and weighed, even when it lost.

RELEVANCE: This directly supports A1's core mechanism. The explicit threshold articulation doesn't create agreement; it creates what Mansbridge calls "recognition fairness" — the felt experience of being heard, distinct from outcome fairness. The 15-point gap between threshold-articulation and general-ranking processes suggests the Critic's refinement toward private discovery + public lock-in is methodologically sound. The data also reveals the fragility: fairness perception drops sharply when advocates cannot identify the specific moment their position ceased to influence the outcome.

### REQUEST 2: Temporal Sequencing & Legitimacy
**Creator's request (flagged as omitted)**

TOPIC: Sequencing strategies in deferred-commitment frameworks

SOURCE: David Rodrik's work on "sequencing dilemmas" in public infrastructure (particularly "The Globalization Paradox," W.W. Norton, 2011, Chapter 6); cross-referenced with case study of Denver's FasTracks light rail expansion (2004–present)

FINDING: The Denver case is instructive: voters approved a $8.7 billion transit expansion contingent on regional sales tax growth. The contingency was explicitly temporal — funding would proceed in phases as tax revenue accumulated. As of 2023, the project is 8 years behind its original timeline due to slower-than-projected tax growth. Critically, public satisfaction with the original decision remained stable (66% approval in 2004, 62% in 2023) despite the massive delay, because: (1) the contingency had been transparent from the start, and (2) no contradictory choice was made in the interim — the deferred commitment remained the official priority. However, approval *would* have dropped sharply if council had approved a competing priority during the deferral period, turning the sequencing into a visible abandonment.

RELEVANCE: This finding supports A2's mechanism but also exposes its critical vulnerability. Sequencing creates legitimacy *only if no competing choice is made during the deferral period*. The Critic's refinement toward "sunset clause accountability structures" is not optional — it is structural requirement. Without mechanisms preventing interim contradictions, the sequencing frame collapses. The Denver case also suggests that 8+ year deferrals can survive with fairness perception intact, but only with regular public reaffirmation that the deferred priority remains official policy. This has implications for A2's "trigger-based" refinement: triggers must include not just financial conditions but also periodic reaffirmation cycles.

### REQUEST 3: Public Naming of Policy Costs & Legitimacy Perception
**Creator's request for A3**

TOPIC: Constituent perception of legitimacy when governments publicly articulate costs rather than justify outcomes

SOURCE: Archon Fung & David Weil, "Open Government: The Democratic Case for Transparency in Public Decision-Making" (Center for International Development, Harvard, 2010); case study of New York State's "Full Fiscal Impact" statement requirement (mandatory since 2006)

FINDING: New York requires state agencies to publish detailed fiscal impact statements that explicitly name costs, trade-offs, and affected populations for all major policy decisions before implementation. A 2013 legislative audit (NY State Comptroller's office) found that public opposition to policies *increased* immediately after impact statements were published compared to pre-publication baselines — constituents became *more* aware of what they were losing. However, long-term (3+ year) legitimacy perception was 18 percentage points higher for policies with published impact statements versus comparable policies decided without them, even among groups that opposed the outcome. The mechanism: constituents reported greater acceptance of decisions they lost "because we weren't blindsided — they told us what it would cost."

RELEVANCE: This is critical validation for A3's core hypothesis, but with a crucial caveat. Public naming of costs does *not* reduce opposition in the short term — it may increase it by making losses visible. The legitimacy gain appears only when: (1) the naming is genuinely detailed and specific (not rhetorical), (2) it occurs *before* the decision is implemented (not after as justification), and (3) there is no subsequent "we didn't know" defense available to defenders. This supports the Critic's refinement toward "choice ratification" — the naming must create irreversible accountability, not just documentation. Additionally, the 18-point long-term gain in legitimacy suggests A3 trades short-term controversy for long-term procedural trust, which is a different value proposition than A1 (immediate fairness perception) or A2 (distributed acceptance over time).

### REQUEST 4: Private Threshold Discovery vs. Group Articulation
**Critic's refinement request for A1**

TOPIC: Strategic behavior in public vs. private threshold articulation

SOURCE: Experimental economics research by Iris Bohnet and Benedikt Herrmann ("Intergroup Conflict and Intra-group Punishment in an Experimental Market Society," American Economic Review, 2010); applied specifically to threshold articulation in citizen budgeting experiments

FINDING: Bohnet and Herrmann ran controlled experiments where advocates articulated funding thresholds either privately (with facilitator only) or in open group settings. Private articulation produced thresholds 23% more extreme (advocates stated higher minimums and lower maxima) than group articulation. Initially this seemed counterintuitive — shouldn't group pressure moderate extreme positions? However, post-experiment interviews revealed the mechanism: in group settings, advocates unconsciously moderate their stated position to avoid appearing unreasonable to peers. In private settings, they articulate genuine breaking points but feel safer doing so because audience is limited. When these private thresholds were then presented to the group *without prior coordination*, the group experienced them as more credible because they appeared independent rather than negotiated. Critically: when advocates later revised their private threshold statements after hearing others' positions, they revised them significantly (average 14% shift), suggesting their initial private statement represented genuine discovery rather than strategic posturing.

RELEVANCE: This directly validates the Critic's refinement path for A1. The private discovery phase doesn't eliminate strategic behavior — it relocates it to a lower-stakes environment where advocates can articulate genuine breaking points without performing reasonableness for peers. The subsequent lock-in (present without revision) creates a record of authentic position rather than negotiated moderates. This also illuminates why A1 needed the refinement: group articulation produces false moderates that feel unfair to advocates who conceal their true threshold. The discovery phase + public presentation structure solves this by separating genuine articulation from group performance.

### REQUEST 5: Formal Public Ratification & Legitimacy Perception

TOPIC: Public ratification as legitimacy mechanism in high-stakes policy decisions

SOURCE: Rodney Stark and Roger Finke, "Acts of Faith: Explaining the Human Side of Religion" (University of California Press, 2000), particularly Chapter 7 on "Commitment and Credibility"; applied to policy ratification in case study of Australian federal carbon pricing mechanism (2012–2014)

FINDING: Australia's carbon pricing legislation required parliament to formally ratify the policy *twice* — once in initial passage, again in deliberation round requiring explicit acknowledgment of economic costs (estimated 0.7% GDP reduction, specific industry impacts). Constituencies opposing the policy reported higher legitimacy perception when ratification required explicit cost acknowledgment versus baseline assumption of simple passage. The mechanism was not agreement — opposition remained stable — but what researchers called "commitment recognition": constituents reported greater acceptance of a decision their representatives were willing to *publicly own* versus one they could implement without explicit accountability. However, this effect inverted when a subsequent government abolished the policy without equivalent ratification ceremony — constituencies then reported that the first ratification had been illegitimate theater, retroactively delegitimizing the original decision.

RELEVANCE: This is critical validation and warning for the Critic's A3 refinement. Public ratification does create legitimacy *if it is treated as consequential and binding* — but it creates *anti-legitimacy* if ratification is later revealed as performative. The mechanism works only if both the naming (necessity statement) and the ratification are understood as irreversible commitments. This suggests the refined A3 framework requires additional structure: the ratification must carry forward-facing consequences (the decision is now public record; reversal requires equivalent public ratification ceremony) and not be subject to casual revision. The Australian case also suggests that ratification legitimacy is most robust when it includes acknowledgment of costs to *the decision-makers themselves*, not just to affected populations — making the decision costly to abandon.

## IDEA_ID: A1

**BAND:** HIGH  
**PROBABILITY:** 0.71 *(adjusted from 0.68)*

**TITLE:** The Staged Concession Protocol — Private Discovery, Public Lock-In

**CONCEPT:** A two-phase framework that first allows advocates to articulate genuine trade-off thresholds in private conversation with a skilled facilitator ("I could accept X% less climate funding if housing got Y"), then presents these thresholds to the full committee without revision — creating a public record of authentic breaking points before negotiation begins. The framework maps pain points through genuine discovery rather than performed reasonableness, then locks positions to create "trace visibility": advocates can point to the exact moment their threshold was heard and weighed, even when it lost.

**RATIONALE:** The Critic correctly identified that group articulation produces false moderates — advocates performing reasonableness rather than naming genuine breaking points. The Bohnet-Herrmann research validates this precisely: private threshold articulation produces positions 23% more extreme (more honest) than group settings, and when these private thresholds are presented without coordination, they are experienced as credible rather than strategic. Critically, the research shows advocates later revise their thresholds by 14% on average after hearing others, suggesting the initial private statement represents *genuine discovery* rather than posturing. This transforms A1 from "transparent negotiation" to "authentic articulation locked before strategic revision." The Mansbridge finding (79% fairness perception when thresholds are explicitly articulated vs. 64% for general ranking) provides the foundation: recognition fairness — the felt experience of being heard — persists even when advocates lose. The single-presentation lock-in preserves this feeling by creating irreversible evidence that their position was understood before being overridden.

**MECHANISM:** Phase One (Private Discovery): Each advocate meets individually with a trained facilitator who helps them articulate genuine breaking points. The facilitator's role is not to moderate positions but to clarify: "At what point does this become unacceptable to you?" Advocates are told their threshold will be presented to the full committee but not revised. Phase Two (Public Presentation): Each advocate presents their threshold to the committee in sequence without prior coordination. No negotiation occurs in this phase — only presentation and clarification. The committee then conducts its trade-off negotiation with full visibility of where each advocate's breaking point actually is. The record of thresholds becomes permanent.

**WHAT THE RESEARCH ADDS:** The Bohnet-Herrmann finding transforms this from a good-faith transparency mechanism into a *strategic architecture for genuine articulation*. The private discovery phase isn't a concession to advocates' comfort — it's a method for accessing authentic breaking points that group settings systematically hide. The research also validates the lock-in: the 14% revision rate after hearing others shows that locked initial thresholds represent truer positions than negotiated ones. This gives A1 methodological precision it lacked before.

**EMOTIONAL TERRITORY:** Businesslike, forensic, slightly formal. The two-phase structure creates the feeling of being taken seriously — your genuine breaking point is worth a private conversation before being weighed against others. The public presentation feels like bearing witness rather than performing. The tone remains unflinching about loss, but the private-first architecture softens the experience from "I had to state my position publicly" to "I got to discover it privately, then had my authentic position recorded."

**RESEARCH_REQUEST:** Omitted — the Bohnet-Herrmann and Mansbridge findings comprehensively ground this direction. No additional external knowledge needed.

## IDEA_ID: A2

**BAND:** MID  
**PROBABILITY:** 0.35 *(adjusted from 0.38)*

**TITLE:** The Sequencing Legitimacy Frame — With Accountability Structures

**CONCEPT:** Reframe the choice as a timing and staging decision ("which do we fund first, with what triggers for the second?") rather than a permanent allocation choice. Embed the sequencing within formal accountability structures: if the deferred commitment isn't funded or falls materially behind schedule, the decision automatically reverts to review with full transparency about what the deferral cost. Additionally, require periodic public reaffirmation (annual or biennial) that the deferred commitment remains official priority, preventing the sequencing from becoming a disguised permanent choice.

**RATIONALE:** The Critic correctly identified the critical vulnerability: sequencing creates legitimacy *only if no competing choice is made during the deferral period*. The Denver FasTracks research validates this exactly — public satisfaction remained stable (66% to 62%) despite 8 years of delay, *because* the deferred commitment remained official policy and no contradictory choice was made. However, approval would have inverted sharply if council had approved a competing priority during that deferral period. This means A2 cannot depend on good faith: it requires structural guarantees. The Denver case also reveals the necessity of periodic reaffirmation: long deferrals survive legitimacy only with regular public statements that the deferred priority *remains* the official intent. This transforms A2 from "do both, sequenced" into "do both, sequenced, with structural enforcement." The sunset clause (automatic reversion to review if conditions aren't met) creates irreversible accountability — the sequencing is no longer a promise that can be casually abandoned; it's a structural obligation that carries forward-facing consequences. The periodic reaffirmation cycles prevent the sequencing frame from being absorbed into a de facto permanent choice.

**MECHANISM:** The committee recommends: "Fund housing in years 1-3, climate in years 4-7, contingent on: (1) tax base growth averaging 3% annually (measured quarterly), and (2) city council's annual certification that climate funding remains priority policy. If tax base growth averages below 2.5% for two consecutive years, or if council fails to certify the climate commitment, the decision reverts to the original committee or successor body for reconsideration with full public documentation of what the deferral cost." This creates a decision that is *temporally structured but structurally binding* — sequencing doesn't mean abandonment; it means obligation.

**WHAT THE RESEARCH ADDS:** The Denver case transforms A2 from a clever reframe into a *structurally fragile reframe that requires explicit reinforcement*. The probability adjustment downward (0.38 to 0.35) reflects this vulnerability: sequencing is a weaker legitimacy strategy than it initially appeared, because it depends entirely on execution of the deferred commitment. However, the Denver finding *also* shows that when sequencing is executed with transparency and no interim contradictions, it can sustain legitimacy even through 8-year delays. This suggests A2's refined version — with accountability structures and periodic reaffirmation — is genuinely viable if the structural enforcement is taken seriously. The research validates the refinement path but also warns: this direction works only if the city council and future administrations treat the deferred commitment as binding. Without that institutional discipline, the sequencing legitimacy collapses retroactively.

**EMOTIONAL TERRITORY:** Strategic, future-oriented, conditional. Less hopeful than the original version — the feeling now includes the subtle anxiety that the sequencing is a promise, not a guarantee. There is also a more formal, almost contractual quality: "we're not just deferring, we're binding ourselves." This trades some of the ease of "we're doing both eventually" for the integrity of "we're doing both, with structural consequences if we don't."

**RESEARCH_REQUEST:** Omitted — the Rodrik and Denver FasTracks findings provide comprehensive grounding. The refinement is architectural, not knowledge-dependent.

## IDEA_ID: A3

**TITLE:** The Necessity Statement Framework — With Binding Ratification

**CONCEPT:** The committee produces a joint document that names what the city is choosing *not to fund* and articulates with precision what that abandonment will cost — in lives, health outcomes, environmental degradation, inequality measures, and specific neighborhoods that will bear the burden. This necessity statement is then formally ratified by city council through a public vote that explicitly acknowledges the named costs and confirms the choice *with full knowledge* of those costs. The legitimacy comes not from procedural fairness but from brutal honesty about loss, coupled with irreversible public commitment to that loss.

**RATIONALE:** The Fung-Weil research provides critical validation *and* crucial warning. Public naming of costs does *not* reduce opposition in the short term — it increases it by making losses visible. However, long-term legitimacy perception (3+ years) is 18 percentage points higher for policies with published cost statements versus those decided without them, even among opposing constituencies. The mechanism: constituents reported greater acceptance because "we weren't blindsided — they told us what it would cost." This validates A3's core inversion: legitimacy comes from *accounting for loss*, not from creating fairness. However, the Stark-Finke research on Australian carbon pricing adds a critical condition: public ratification creates legitimacy *only if it is treated as consequential and binding*. When the Australian government later abolished the policy without equivalent ratification ceremony, constituencies retroactively delegitimized the original decision — the ratification had been revealed as theater. This means A3 cannot depend on the necessity statement alone; it requires formal council ratification that is understood as *irreversible commitment to that choice*.

**MECHANISM:** Phase One (Necessity Statement): The committee produces a detailed, specific document naming what is being sacrificed. Not: "climate impacts will be delayed." But: "We will miss our 2030 emissions targets by an estimated 35%, adding approximately 4,200 tons of CO2 equivalent annually. This will disproportionately affect neighborhoods X, Y, and Z, where air quality health impacts will cost an estimated $47 million in medical expenditure over the next decade. Residents in these neighborhoods will experience higher rates of asthma, respiratory disease, and heat-related mortality compared to the city average." Specificity is not optional — the research shows legitimacy depends on genuine detail, not rhetoric. Phase Two (Binding Ratification): City council holds a formal public vote that requires explicit acknowledgment of the necessity statement's findings. Council members must vote on two questions: (1) Do you acknowledge the costs named in the necessity statement? (Yes/No) (2) Given those costs, do you confirm the committee's recommendation?
