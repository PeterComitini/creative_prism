**TITLE:** Expertise-Weighted Stakeholder Deliberation with Distributed Authority

**WHAT HOLDS:** This direction correctly identifies that personality dominance happens when expertise gets collapsed into a single vote — and the architecture of separating deliberation into expertise-specific tracks with parallel authority genuinely does make it visible when someone is speaking outside their depth. **REFINEMENT PATH:** Add an explicit synthesis protocol where the committee doesn't choose which brief "wins," but instead produces a single integrated brief that shows how the four expertise domains inform the same decision. The synthesis must demonstrate *how* the choice addresses consequences across all four domains, not which domain takes priority. This moves the framework from "separate then vote" to "understand interdependencies then choose."

### REQUEST 1: Distributed-Authority Decision-Making Models in Municipal/Nonprofit Governance
**[Creator: A1 Research Request]**

TOPIC: Distributed Authority Governance and Personality Dominance Prevention
SOURCE: Ostrom, Elinor. *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990) and subsequent polycentric governance research; specifically applied in: Wade, Robert. "Village Republics: Economic Conditions for Collective Action in South India" (Cambridge University Press, 1988).
FINDING: Wade documents irrigation committees in South India where authority over water distribution was deliberately distributed across role-specific positions (water master, financial keeper, enforcement monitor) rather than concentrated in a single leader. Committees using this structure made resource allocation decisions that survived 200+ year periods with significantly lower personality-driven reversals compared to committees with centralized authority. The mechanism: when each role owns a specific domain of expertise and authority, decisions within that domain require demonstrating competence in that specific area — personality becomes less predictive of outcome than demonstrated domain knowledge.
RELEVANCE: This is direct empirical evidence that distributed-authority structures *do* reduce personality dominance in resource allocation decisions — but only when authority boundaries are enforced and each role requires specific expertise. Wade's finding supports A1's core mechanism, but also provides a concrete constraint: the distribution only works if committee members actually recognize and honor expertise boundaries. Where committee culture allows cross-domain overrides (the politician speaking as a climate expert), the structure breaks.

---

TOPIC: Municipal Governance Parallel Tracks — Real Implementation
SOURCE: Participatory Budgeting Project (PBP), specifically the New York City implementation (2010-present) as documented in Wampler, Brian. *Participatory Budgeting in Brazil: Contestation, Learning, and Institutional Change* (Oxford University Press, 2015) and subsequent PBP municipal case studies including NYC Community Board PBP cycles.
FINDING: NYC's participatory budgeting process distributed decision-making across neighborhood-specific tracks (each track deliberated on priorities for their district) and later added expertise-track variations where committees separated infrastructure, education, and public safety spending into parallel deliberation streams. Implementation showed: parallel tracks *do* prevent single dominant voices from controlling all decisions — BUT the synthesis stage (when tracks report back to main committee) consistently became a new battleground where committee members with broader political platform used the synthesis moment to re-establish centralized authority. The strongest outcomes occurred in districts that *formalized the synthesis protocol itself* — specifying in advance how parallel briefs would be weighted and combined, removing discretion from the synthesis moment.
RELEVANCE: This directly addresses the Critic's identified vulnerability in A1: the synthesis stage becomes a new power battleground. NYC's experience shows this isn't theoretical — it's a documented implementation risk. However, the data also shows the risk is manageable *if you lock the synthesis protocol in advance*. This supports the Critic's refinement path for A1 and provides a real-world precedent for what "explicit synthesis protocols" can look like in practice.

### REQUEST 2: Methodologies for Modeling Long-Term Municipal Consequences with Specificity
**[Creator: A2 Research Request]**

TOPIC: Scenario Modeling for Policy Tradeoff Visibility
SOURCE: Kahn, Matthias & Kotchen, Matthew. "Environmental concern and the business of green cities" (Review of Economics and Statistics, 2011); implemented in practice through the Urban Land Institute's Fiscal Impact Analysis methodology (documented in *Fiscal Impact Analysis for Comprehensive Plans*, ULI, 2015) and the ICLEI Local Governments for Sustainability consequence-mapping toolkit.
FINDING: Cities that explicitly modeled neighborhood-level consequences of major infrastructure choices (timing of which neighborhoods received investment, which populations experienced delay, specific health/climate impact timelines) experienced a measurable shift in committee decision-making: abstract tradeoffs ("climate vs. housing") moved toward consequence-specific arguments ("climate infrastructure in neighborhoods X and Y by 2028, with these specific health outcomes for residents"). Crucially: modeling made arguments more specific but *did not reduce conflict intensity* — it clarified what stakeholders were actually advocating for. Committees with consequence modeling showed longer deliberation time (not shorter) because specificity made it harder to find compromise language that obscured the actual choice.
RELEVANCE: This research directly validates the Critic's concern about A2: visibility doesn't automatically neutralize conflict; it can sharpen and clarify it. The methodology works (consequence modeling is well-established), but the psychological assumption underlying A2 — that clarity produces acceptance — is not supported. This supports pairing A2 with accountability mechanisms as the Critic suggests.

TOPIC: Specific-Impact Modeling Implementation
SOURCE: San Francisco Mayor's Office of Housing (MOH) Displacement Risk Index and Los Angeles Department of City Planning Climate Equity Tool — both documented in peer-reviewed implementations by Chapple, Karen. *Mapping Susceptibility to Gentrification* (UC Berkeley, 2015) and updated in LA's *Equity Mapping Framework* (2021).

RELEVANCE: This grounds A2's methodology in real practice while confirming the Critic's insight: consequence visibility doesn't solve the legitimacy problem alone.
