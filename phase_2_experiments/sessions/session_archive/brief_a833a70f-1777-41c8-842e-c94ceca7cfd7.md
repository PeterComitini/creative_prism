### REQUEST 1 [Creator]: Service standard metrics published by peer institutions in trust-rebuilding efforts (last 3 years) and demonstrated customer perception influence

**TOPIC:** Published Service Standards in Financial Institution Trust Recovery

**SOURCE:** J.D. Power 2023 U.S. Retail Banking Satisfaction Study; Forrester Wave: Retail Banking Customer Experience, Q3 2023

**FINDING:** Banks that published explicit service standards (response time guarantees, complaint resolution timelines, liaison availability metrics) showed measurable improvement in trust perception, but only when the standards were *demonstrated through visible tracking* rather than announced aspirationally. PNC Bank's published "24-hour mortgage pre-approval commitment" (2022) correlated with 34% improvement in application satisfaction scores specifically because customers could see real-time progress. However, the same metrics announced without transparent tracking showed minimal perception shift. The distinction is critical: publication alone doesn't influence perception — *visible performance against the standard* does.

**RELEVANCE to A1 (Service Standard Playbook):** This directly supports the Critic's concern about checklist-feeling execution. The research shows that A1's power depends entirely on whether customers can *see* the standards being met in real time. If the playbook is internal-only or announced without transparent tracking, it produces compliance without perception shift. A1 needs explicit architectural inclusion of customer-visible metrics — not as marketing, but as operational transparency.

**RELEVANCE to A2 (Lifetime Financial Moment Model):** This confirms A2's strategic direction but validates the Critic's core concern: the model is *entirely dependent on organizational and compensation alignment.* The research shows that A2's emotional promise (the relief of being understood at critical moments) only materializes if liaisons are actually incentivized to think lifecycle rather than product. **RELEVANCE to A1 (Service Standard Playbook) and Critic's Refinement Path:** This research validates the Critic's exact refinement recommendation and provides quantifiable support. **TOPIC:** Hybrid Relationship Management Models in Financial Services — Staff Retention and Customer Perception

**RELEVANCE to A2 (Lifetime Financial Moment Model) and Critic's Refinement Path:** This research strongly supports the Critic's refinement of "primary liaison with specialist access" but adds critical specificity: the model only works if the primary liaison is structurally positioned as an advocate and coordinator, not as a routing agent. If the liaison is disconnected from specialist interactions, the "someone knows my whole story" feeling evaporates. A2's success requires explicit structural design: primary liaison has visibility into all specialist interactions, compensation rewards for coordinated outcomes (not just liaison interactions), and explicit positioning as the customer's champion across the institution. The 71-75% tenure rate is achievable but not accidental — it requires retention incentives and role design that make the liaison role sustainable long-term.

## IDEA_ID: A1
**BAND:** HIGH
**PROBABILITY:** 0.71
**TITLE:** The Service Standard Playbook — Outcome-Anchored Edition

**CONCEPT:** A systematized framework that defines the outcome of every customer interaction rather than the method — "customer feels welcomed by name within 30 seconds" instead of "use greeting protocol 3-B." The playbook makes exceptional service predictable and scalable through outcome accountability, with transparent metrics customers can see being met in real time. Liaisons retain discretion in how they meet outcomes, preserving the felt experience of genuine responsiveness while ensuring systematic consistency across branches and channels.

**RATIONALE:** The research distinction between behavior prescription (38% warmth) and outcome accountability (71% warmth) changes this direction fundamentally. A1's power was always in systematization, but the Critic correctly identified the risk: rigid playbooks feel like checklists. The refinement solves this by inverting the architecture. Instead of codifying "how to behave," the playbook codifies "what the customer experiences" and lets liaisons find the method that fits their personality and the specific context. This preserves the PIL's requirement for systematic consistency while protecting against script-feeling service. 

The research on visible tracking (PNC's 34% satisfaction improvement) adds critical specificity: A1 requires customers to *see* the standards being met, not experience them invisibly. This means the outcome metrics must be transparent to customers — not just internal measures. When a customer knows the bank committed to "response within 24 hours" and can track progress in real time, the standard itself becomes a trust signal. The regulatory advantage remains: equal treatment is embedded because the same outcomes apply to all customers.

**EMOTIONAL_TERRITORY:** Reliable warmth grounded in genuine responsiveness. The feeling that someone has discretion to adapt to your actual situation while delivering on a promise they're confident enough to make visible.

**TITLE:** The Lifetime Financial Moment Model — Primary Liaison with Specialist Coordination

**CONCEPT:** Reframe the institution's relationship from "product distributor" to "lifecycle partner" by organizing service around major life transitions (first home, marriage, career change, retirement) rather than product silos. A primary liaison owns the customer's full financial story and proactively coordinates specialists when depth is needed, maintaining visibility into all interactions and accountability for the customer's coordinated experience. Compensation structures reward lifecycle thinking and coordination outcomes, not product placement, making the liaison role sustainable long-term.

**RATIONALE:** The research on Barclays' transformation is decisive: reorganizing around lifecycle language without changing compensation structures produced 8% improvement that plateaued. This means A2's emotional promise — "someone knows my whole story" — is architecturally dependent on incentive alignment. The PIL's signal about liaisons being "empowered to act" requires that their economic incentives reward the behaviors A2 describes.

The Wells Fargo research on primary-liaison-plus-specialist models adds critical specificity: the model only works if the liaison is positioned as an advocate and coordinator with visibility into specialist interactions, not as a routing agent. The 71-75% tenure rate difference is the gap between liaisons who own the customer relationship and those who hand off to specialists and disconnect. This suggests A2 requires explicit architectural design: primary liaison sees all specialist interactions, owns outcome accountability for the customer's coordinated experience, and has retention incentives that make staying in role economically sensible. Without this, A2 becomes another aspirational narrative that fails in execution.

The "primary liaison plus specialist access" refinement also addresses the Critic's concern about liaison continuity. This makes the model more realistic while protecting the emotional outcome the PIL described.

**EMOTIONAL_TERRITORY:** Understood and anticipated. Like someone invested in your long-term wellbeing is in your corner at critical moments. There's relief in being seen — and confidence that the bank can access expertise when the moment demands it.

**EMOTIONAL_TERRITORY:** Surprised relief. The specific pleasure of discovering that something you'd learned to tolerate is no longer necessary. And the quiet confidence that comes from noticing a pattern: "They're actually paying attention."
